02 · curriculum building blocks

Space. Time.
Dynamics.

You don't need choreography. You just need the elements!
Tap any card to expand and explore.

SPACE · levels, dimension, direction +
  • Levels: low, middle, high
  • Dimension: big movement, small movement, tall, quiet
  • Shape: straight, smooth, rounded, angled, symmetrical, asymmetrical
  • Direction: forward, backwards, sideways, diagonal
  • Personal space: the space each person needs to complete movements
TIME · tempo & rhythm +
  • Metre: time signature (4/4 standard 8 counts, 3/4 waltz)
  • Tempo: speed of movement (slow, fast, accelerating, decelerating)
  • Rhythm: patterns of movement
  • Phrasing: organisation/grouping of movement
  • Accents: emphasis on a particular movement
DYNAMICS · explosive vs sustained +
  • Weight/force: how body weight is used to develop movement quality
  • Energy/explosion: the lead-up and strong release of energy
  • Quality: how well movement is developed or sustained
MOVEMENT · the physical act +

Movement is the foundation - the act of the body in motion. All other elements describe how, where, and when that movement happens.

STRUCTURES · Narrative, Repetition, Variation, motif +
  • Narrative: beginning, middle, end (ABC structure)
  • Repetition: repeated portions (ABA, ABACA)
  • Variation: similar movements with changes
  • Motif: a main phrase shown with variations
FORMS · ideas & entry points +

Visual, auditory, ideational stimuli · Exploration and improvisation

RELATIONSHIPS · solo, pair, group, unison +
  • Students might visualise a story or character
  • They may hear specific parts and associate movement
Teacher Insight

Why Dance is a Vital Classroom Tool

Dance is a powerful "classroom hack" for social and emotional learning. It helps students manage big emotions, promotes pro-social behavior like cooperation, and provides a structured channel for energy that can help students with ADHD improve focus and readiness to learn.

03 · research

The why.

Teacher anxiety about teaching dance is well documented in the literature, with many preservice and practising teachers feeling underprepared due to limited prior experience or subject-specific knowledge. Research consistently shows that confidence and competence in dance education improve when preservice teachers receive strong theoretical preparation alongside practical, hands-on experience during their initial teacher education (ITE) programmes (Hennessy et al., 2001; Rolfe, 2001).

Teacher anxiety surrounding the delivery of dance education is recognised as a global issue; however, it is not insurmountable. Many preservice primary teachers enter ITE programmes with limited background in dance and low confidence in their ability to teach it. Russell-Bowie (2013) conducted a cross-national study of primary preservice teachers across five countries and identified an urgent need for ITE programmes to increase both competence and confidence in dance education. Across multiple studies, a clear trend emerges: preservice teacher confidence grows when high-quality theoretical preparation is combined with meaningful practical experience in school settings.

Hennessy et al. (2001) and Rolfe (2001) both point to the importance of strong professional experience placements, where preservice teachers can observe effective teaching practice and receive constructive feedback from supervising teachers. These experiences help normalise dance as part of the primary curriculum and chip away at the perception that specialist expertise is required to deliver it effectively. Although much of this foundational research is older, it continues to be widely cited because contemporary empirical work on teacher confidence in dance remains limited, though recent studies have begun to fill that gap.

A systematic review by Shen et al. (2026) underlines how central teacher capability and ongoing pedagogical support are to improving outcomes in dance education. The review identified several practical strategies for building confidence and competence, including: developing sufficient subject-matter knowledge, building positive relationships with students, identifying individual student needs, selecting appropriate teaching methods, guiding students effectively, and establishing a supportive classroom environment. The authors found that confidence develops when teachers feel equipped with both content knowledge and the tools to teach dance safely and with clear purpose, rather than simply being familiar with the art form.

Within the NSW context, the Creative Arts K–6 Syllabus (NESA, 2024) positions Dance as one of four mandatory art forms, requiring generalist primary teachers to plan and deliver learning experiences across Composing, Performing, and Appreciating. These expectations place real responsibility on teachers, many of whom report limited prior experience or confidence in dance. When teachers feel underprepared, their capacity to meet syllabus requirements suffers. When they receive adequate preparation and support, through both ITE programmes and ongoing professional learning, they are better placed to teach dance with confidence and a clear sense of purpose.

Accessible professional learning resources can also support practising teachers in building confidence outside of formal ITE contexts. The Teach NSW Podcast (Season 2, Episode 15) features Marianne, an experienced arts educator and Chief Education Officer at The Arts Unit, who argues for building 'arts confidence' rather than focusing on technical 'arts skills,' encouraging teachers who feel unsure to step outside their comfort zone and simply try. Resources like this reinforce a key finding across the literature: building teacher confidence in dance does not require specialist expertise. It starts with a willingness to have a go.

Key takeaways: What teachers need

  • ✓ Clear subject knowledge (the Elements)
    Understanding Space, Time, Dynamics removes the mystery from dance teaching
  • ✓ Practical strategies (not perfection)
    Focus on improvisation circles, mirroring, scaffolded sequences vs polished choreography
  • ✓ Supportive resources (like this DLP)
    Access to ready-made materials and evidence-based approaches builds capability

EAL/D Learners

For students learning English as an Additional Language or Dialect (EAL/D), two key approaches support participation in dance: multimodal representation and explicit vocabulary instruction through scaffolding and modelling. The Australian Curriculum, Assessment and Reporting Authority (ACARA, 2011) notes that multimodal representations are vital for EAL/D learners to make sense of learning tasks. In dance, visual dance action cards can communicate movement concepts without relying on language alone. The NSW Department of Education (2026) also highlights scaffolding and modelling as essential strategies for EAL/D students, including breaking dance sequences into smaller steps and using explicit instruction and repetition to build understanding and confidence progressively.

Students with Physical, Cognitive, Social, and Emotional Considerations

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) offers a practical framework for making dance accessible to all students. Almeqdad et al. (2023) found that UDL supports teachers in reducing barriers through flexible and differentiated curriculum design. The Australian Education Research Organisation (AERO, 2024) describes reasonable adjustments as measures that help students with disability access and take part in learning. In dance, these might include simplified or adapted movements, tasks broken into smaller steps, and mirroring or modelling to give students a low-risk way into the activity. These approaches help build confidence, participation, and a sense of belonging across the class.

High-Potential and Gifted Students

The NSW Department of Education (2025) points to differentiation, flexible grouping, enrichment, and acceleration as the main strategies for supporting high-potential and gifted students. In dance, these students tend to thrive when given complex, open-ended choreographic tasks that give them more ownership over their learning and push them further across Composing, Performing, and Appreciating (NESA, 2024). Access to expert support through extension opportunities and extracurricular programmes aligned with syllabus outcomes provides more advanced learning pathways, keeping gifted students challenged and progressing.

Reluctant Learners

Reluctant learners in dance (students who are disengaged, unmotivated, or anxious about taking part) tend to respond well to a mix of student choice and scaffolded entry points. Letting students make decisions about music, movement style, and how they want to express their ideas taps into what they already enjoy and do well. The NSW Department of Education (2025) notes that promoting student autonomy is a key lever for improving engagement. The Australian Education Research Organisation (AERO, 2024) adds that scaffolding reduces unnecessary cognitive load, which in turn lifts motivation and helps students complete tasks.

05 · culture & connection

Consultation,
not imitation.

A significant source of teacher anxiety is the fear of "getting it wrong" when teaching Aboriginal, Torres Strait Islander, or other cultural dances. The key is Consultation..

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander People in the Classroom
Presented by Reconciliation Australia: A guide to authentic engagement and diverse First Nations perspectives in education.



Practical Steps

01
AECG Consultation

Reach out to your local Aboriginal Education Consultative Group. They can connect you with local Elders or dance groups who can visit the school.

02
Focus on Appreciating

If you cannot have a guest, use high-quality digital resources like Narragunnawali. Watch performances and ask students to observe the connection between movement and the environment.

03
Identify Language Groups

Always identify the specific language group of the dance you are studying. Use the Austlang database to research specific groups. This teaches students that Aboriginal culture is diverse and localised, not monolithic.

04
Cross-Curricular Integration

Use dance to explore history (HSIE) and storytelling (English), ensuring that cultural dance is seen as a living, breathing form of communication.
